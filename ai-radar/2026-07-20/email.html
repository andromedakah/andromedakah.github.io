The Signet Ring

Monday, 20 July 2026 · Cross-sector edition · C-level + Engineering

Yesterday the value settled on the triage tent — the routing layer (which model does the work). Today it moves one turn deeper: not which model, but how much authority the work may carry. In two weeks, three jurisdictions converged on one demand — an autonomous agent acting in your name must operate under a defined, graduated, logged grant of authority. China's world-first agent rules (in force 15 July) mandate a three-tier decision-authorization framework; Illinois signed a frontier-audit law (6 July); the EU AI Act enforces GPAI in 13 days (2 Aug). An autonomous agent is a signet ring you hand to a machine — decide the seal in advance, keep the officer present, keep the roll.

1 · Executive Summary

The governance thesis moves from routing to authorization — and this week that layer became law. China's Implementation Opinions on intelligent agents (CAC/NDRC/MIIT) took effect 15 July — the world's first dedicated regulatory category for AI agents — mandating a three-tier decision-authorization framework that scales human sign-off to the consequence of each action. Illinois signed the first US state frontier-audit law (SB 315, 6 July). The EU AI Act's GPAI enforcement is applicable 2 Aug (13 days; €15M or 3% of turnover). Beneath: Kimi K3 open weights 27 July (7 days), MCP final spec 28 July (8 days).

1. An agent acts in your name — three regulators just made that a governed act. China defines an agent by "autonomous perception, memory, decision-making, interaction, and execution" and requires a three-tier grant: routine acts alone, consequential acts need a human, grave acts need a scaled senior sign-off — plus filing, compliance testing and product-recall for high-risk sectors (healthcare, transport, media, public safety). The signet, the privy seal, the Great Seal — written as statute.

2. The value (and the compliance) is the authorization layer, not the model. Gartner still expects 40% of enterprise apps to carry agents by end-2026 and >40% of agentic projects cancelled by 2027. The survivors can state and prove how much authority each agent holds. Yesterday's triage tent sorted which model; today's signet ring sorts which decisions an agent may make alone.

3. A grant you don't record isn't a grant — it's an exposure. Each regime pairs authorization with a record: China's filing and recall, Illinois's 72-hour incident reporting and annual third-party audit, the EU's powers to compel documentation (Art. 91), run evaluations (Art. 92) and demand mitigations (Art. 93). The authorization layer is the only place that sees every act — so it's the only place you can keep the roll.

Bottom line: when an agent can perceive, decide and execute autonomously, "which model" is a commodity question; "how much authority, and who signs off when it matters" is the one three regulators now enforce. Decide the grant in advance. Scale the sign-off to the consequence. Keep the roll — and keep the power to break the ring.

2 · Allegory — The Signet Ring

For most of history, a ruler who couldn't be in two places governed through a seal: a signet ring pressed into wax made a document act as if the sovereign's own hand had signed it. So the seal was never one thing casually lent. The English crown kept a ladder of seals — routine business under the signet and privy seal (a clerk could act), but the gravest acts — a treaty, a grant of land, a writ that could end a life — required the Great Seal of the Realm, held by the Lord Chancellor and applied with ceremony. The higher the consequence, the higher the seal, and the more senior the human who had to be present.

Two things made it trustworthy, and both are what people forget: every sealed act was entered on a roll (the Chancery's Patent and Close Rolls — a permanent record of who acted under which seal), and the grant could be revoked — a seal recalled from a deputy who abused it, and the Great Seal ceremonially defaced at a reign's end so no one could act in the dead king's name. An autonomous agent is a signet ring you hand to a machine. China's three-tier framework is the crown's ladder rewritten for software: routine acts under the signet (agent alone), consequential under the privy seal (a human approves), the gravest under the Great Seal (senior sign-off) — with a filing that is the roll and a recall power that is the breaking of the ring.

The question it forces: When an agent acts in our name, have we defined the ladder of seals — which decisions alone, which need a human, which need senior sign-off — scaled to the consequence, as China requires and the EU enforces in 13 days? And do we keep the roll of every act taken under our seal — and can we break the ring the moment we need to?

3 · Technical — The Ladder, the Officer, the Roll

Strip the jurisdictions apart and China, Illinois and the EU converge on one claim: when a machine acts autonomously in your name, the governed object is the grant of authority, not the model. The ladder (graduated authorization — the control): the same frontier model can run at any tier; your ladder, not the vendor's, decides how much authority a task carries. The officer (human sign-off, present by design): a named human on the top tier — an enforced checkpoint the agent can't pass alone, the EU's "human oversight" duty. The roll (the record): the one vantage point that sees every act, so it's the only place to log which agent, which action, under which seal — your evidence for Illinois's 72-hour report, China's filing and the EU's documentation powers — and what makes revocation auditable.

On legacy: same seam this radar keeps arriving at — own what doesn't commoditize (6 Jul metering, 7 Jul routing, 8 Jul MCP, … 18 Jul the aluminum age, 19 Jul the triage tent). Today, above routing: the signet ring. Commodity models (Kimi K3, 7 days), standard plumbing (MCP, 8 days) and cheap routing all mean more agents, more actions, more autonomously — every one a decision in your name. Scarcity used to govern how much authority an agent wielded; make agents cheap and continuous and that governor is gone, which is why three regulators are moving it into law. The retrofit: a decision-authorization ladder, an enforced human checkpoint on the top tier, a roll (doubling as your EU AI Act evidence), and a tested break-the-ring path wired to every agent identity.

5 · So What — 3 Moves for the Next 30 Days

1. Draw the ladder of seals — per agent, before the next deploy. Classify each agent's actions by consequence into three tiers: what it may do alone (routine, reversible), what needs a human (consequential), and what needs a senior, named sign-off (grave, irreversible). This is what China now requires you to document and enforce before deployment — and it's a property of your authorization layer, not the model, so it survives every model swap.

2. Put the officer on the top tier, and keep the roll. Wire an enforced human checkpoint the agent can't pass alone — present by design, not a swipe-away alert — and log every consequential act (which agent, which action, under which seal, whose sign-off). That roll is your evidence for Illinois's 72-hour report, China's filing, and the EU's documentation powers on 2 August (13 days) — one build, three regimes.

3. Build the break-the-ring path now. A grant you can't revoke isn't governance. Before enforcement lands, make sure you can pull any agent's authority in minutes — a kill-switch wired to agent identity, tested — and name who can pull it. Break the ring on purpose and on the record, not in a scramble.
