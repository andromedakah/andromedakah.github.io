1 · Executive Summary

The governance thesis reaches its verdict: value. After a month of building the stack (meter, router, MCP, authority, identity), current coverage says enterprise AI's centre of gravity has moved from models to orchestration, governance and ROI clarity — and the CFO arrived with a budget freeze. The reckoning is data: MIT — 95% of GenAI pilots deliver no measurable P&L; Gartner — 89% of agent pilots never reach production, >40% of agentic projects cancelled by end-2027; BCG — only 26% report meaningful financial value; PwC — 56% of CEOs saw neither more revenue nor less cost (only 12% got both); S&P Global — 42% abandoned most AI projects; Deloitte — only 4% report AI value to the board. Beneath: Kimi K3 open weights 27 July (5 days), MCP final spec 28 July (6 days), EU AI Act GPAI enforcement 2 Aug (11 days).

1. The centre of gravity moved from the model to the return. A month ago: which model, at what price. Now: did the money come back. Forrester says firms are postponing ~25% of planned AI spend to 2027 until returns show. The seed is nearly free (Kimi K3, 5 days); nobody pays for seed anymore — they ask for grain.

2. Pilots are not a harvest. 95% of GenAI pilots produce no P&L (MIT); 89% never reach production (Gartner); 42% have abandoned most AI work (S&P Global). MIT: "pilots lie — clean data, isolated systems, goodwill; production brings ownership, integration and accountability." That production layer is the stack you built — but it only pays if pointed at a named outcome.

3. The few that reach the scale pay — and almost no one weighs the crop. The minority reaching production reportedly returns ~171% ROI (as reported); banking/insurance run 47% in production vs 14–18% for gov/health (S&P/McKinsey). Yet only 4% report AI value to the board (Deloitte) and <1 in 3 can name a financial outcome (Gartner).

Bottom line: the seed (the model) is commoditizing to free; what you own is the land, irrigation, market access and ledger — worthless until grain reaches the scale. Stop funding acres planted (pilots); demand bushels delivered (P&L outcomes). Plant every agent against a named outcome and owner. Kill the dry rows. Irrigate the yield. Weigh the crop on the board's scale — and enter it in the book.

2 · Allegory — The Harvest

Every spring the same temptation: seed is cheap and it costs almost nothing to plant. So an anxious lord plants everything and admires the green. But a field of green is not a harvest — the only number that ever mattered was the one taken months later at the granary scale: bushels weighed, entered in the book. Four disciplines separated the estate that ate all winter from the one that starved among its green fields: it planted for the market (against a buyer and price known in advance), it tended and thinned (pulling dry rows without sentiment), it irrigated the few that yielded, and — the part every lord skips — it weighed the crop and wrote the number in the book.

For a month you built the finest estate in the county — metered the water, sorted the fields, walled the boundary, tagged every hand. But this season you planted a thousand pilots and weighed almost nothing. The green is spectacular; the numbers at the granary door are brutal — 95 rows in 100 set no grain (MIT), 89 seedlings in 100 never reach the harvest (Gartner), only 4 estates in 100 even bring a scale to the board (Deloitte). The seed you fretted over is about to be given away (Kimi K3, 5 days). It was never the seed — it was always the harvest.

The question it forces: We planted a whole field of pilots — but can we name, for each agent, the P&L outcome it was sown against and who owns it? Have we pulled the dry rows, or are we still watering them? And when the board asks what the land returned, do we carry a weighed bushel to the scale — or a photograph of a green field?

3 · Technical — The Seed, the Yield Gap, the Scale

When the seed is free, the governed object is no longer the model — it is the harvest and the scale you weigh it on. The seed (the model): commoditizing to free — Kimi K3 open weights in 5 days lower the cost of planting to near zero, which is why estates drown in green pilots. The yield gap (why pilots don't scale — not the model): MIT — "pilots lie: clean data, isolated systems, goodwill; production brings ownership, integration, accountability." The gap between green field and sold grain is the production stack you built (meter, router, MCP, authority, identity) — but it only pays when each agent is planted against a named P&L outcome and owner. The scale (measurement): only 4% report AI value to the board; <1 in 3 can name a financial outcome — build the outcome ledger (each agent → a P&L line, owner, baseline, weighed result).

5 · So What — 3 Moves for the Next 30 Days

1. Plant for the market — tie every agent to a P&L outcome and owner before you sow. No new agent enters production without a named financial line (cost out or revenue in), a baseline and a single owner. This separates the 5% extracting value from the 95% stuck in green — and doubles as your EU AI Act evidence and board report. The 4% who report value to the board keep their budgets when the CFO's freeze (Forrester: ~25%) reaches you.

2. Tend and thin — cull the dry rows now, not in a panic later. Walk every pilot this month and pull the ones with no path to a weighed outcome. 42% of firms have already been forced to abandon most AI work; do it on your terms and reclaim the water for the fields setting grain. Beware agent washing — of thousands of "agentic" vendors, Gartner counts ~130 that truly deliver.

3. Irrigate the yield and weigh the crop. Concentrate spend on the ~11% already reaching production (~171% ROI as reported; ~4-month payback in customer service). Build the outcome ledger — each agent to a P&L line, owner, baseline and weighed result — and carry that number, not a demo, to the board.
