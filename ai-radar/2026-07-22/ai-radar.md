## 1 · Executive Summary (90-second read)

For four weeks this radar has argued one thesis — *the model is commoditizing; own and govern the layer around it* — and built the stack that proves it, one layer a day: metering, routing, the MCP plane, the authorization ladder (the signet ring), the identity passport. Today the argument reaches its only possible verdict: **value.** Current July-2026 coverage frames the shift bluntly — enterprise AI's **centre of gravity is moving from models to orchestration, governance and ROI clarity** — and the CFO has arrived with a budget freeze. The reckoning is now data, not vibes: **MIT's** GenAI-Divide study finds **95%** of generative-AI pilots deliver **no measurable P&L**; **Gartner** finds **89%** of agent pilots never reach production and predicts **>40%** of agentic-AI projects will be **cancelled by end-2027**; **BCG** (1,800 execs) finds only **26%** have generated meaningful financial value; **PwC's** Davos CEO survey finds **56%** saw neither more revenue nor less cost from AI last year (only **12%** got both); **S&P Global** finds **42%** have abandoned most AI projects; **Deloitte** finds only **4%** report AI value to the board. The standing countdowns run beneath, one day closer: **Kimi K3's open weights on 27 July (5 days),** **MCP's final spec on 28 July (6 days),** and the **EU AI Act's GPAI enforcement on 2 August (11 days).**

1. **The centre of gravity moved from the model to the return.** A month ago the debate was which model, at what price. Now it is *did the money come back.* Coverage this week calls it a shift "from models to orchestration, governance and ROI clarity," and CFOs are enforcing it — **Forrester** says enterprises are **postponing ~25% of planned AI spend to 2027** until returns are shown. The seed is nearly free (Kimi K3, 5 days); nobody is paying for seed anymore — they are asking for grain.

2. **Pilots are not a harvest — and most sown seed never becomes sold grain.** **95%** of GenAI pilots produce no measurable P&L (MIT); **89%** of agent pilots never reach production (Gartner); **42%** of firms have already abandoned most of their AI work (S&P Global). MIT's diagnosis is not about the model: *"pilots lie — they run on clean data, isolated systems and goodwill; production introduces ownership, security, integration, edge cases and accountability."* That production layer is exactly the stack this radar spent a month building — but it only pays if it is pointed at a named outcome.

3. **The few that reach the scale pay handsomely — and almost no one is weighing the crop.** The minority of pilots that reach production reportedly return on the order of **171% ROI** (industry coverage, as reported), and banking/insurance already run agents in production at **47%** vs **14–18%** for government/healthcare (S&P Global/McKinsey). Yet only **4%** of organizations report AI value to the board (Deloitte) and **fewer than one-third** of decision-makers can name a specific financial outcome from their AI spend (Gartner). You cannot manage a harvest you never weigh.

**Bottom line:** the seed (the model) is commoditizing to free; what you own is the **land, the irrigation, the market access and the ledger** — but none of it is worth anything until grain reaches the scale. Stop funding **acres planted** (pilots launched) and start demanding **bushels delivered** (outcomes booked to the P&L). **Plant every agent against a named P&L outcome and owner. Kill the dry rows early. Irrigate the few that yield. Weigh the crop on the board's scale — and enter it in the book.**

## 2 · Allegory of the Day — "The Harvest"

*Topic: This week's coverage says enterprise AI's centre of gravity has shifted from models to ROI clarity, and CFOs are imposing budget controls — with Forrester reporting that firms are postponing about a quarter of planned AI spend to 2027 until returns are demonstrated. The underpinning data is a reckoning: MIT finds 95% of generative-AI pilots deliver no measurable P&L; Gartner finds 89% of agent pilots never reach production and expects more than 40% of agentic-AI projects to be cancelled by end-2027; BCG finds only 26% of firms have generated meaningful financial value; PwC finds 56% of CEOs saw neither higher revenue nor lower cost from AI last year; and Deloitte finds only 4% report AI value to the board. Meanwhile the model itself keeps commoditizing — Kimi K3's open weights publish in five days. The lesson for the enterprise: when the seed is free, the estate is judged not by how much it plants but by how much it harvests, weighs, and brings to market.*

Every spring the same temptation visits the estate: seed is cheap, the ground is soft, and it costs almost nothing to *plant.* So an anxious lord plants everything — every field, every margin, every strip of poor soil — and stands back to admire the green. Planting feels like progress. It photographs well. It fills the estate with the appearance of a coming abundance. And it is almost entirely worthless, because a field of green is not a harvest. The only number that ever mattered was the one taken months later, at the scale by the granary door: **bushels weighed, and entered in the book.** A wise steward knew the cruelest fact of farming — that most sown seed never becomes sold grain. Drought, blight, poor soil, and the plain arithmetic of the market take the rest. The green field is a promise; the weighed bushel is a return.

Four disciplines separated the estate that ate all winter from the one that starved among its own green fields. First, it **planted for the market, not for the plot** — every field was sown against a buyer and a price known in advance, not simply because there was seed and soil. Second, it **tended and thinned** — the steward walked the rows in midsummer and pulled up the dry ones without sentiment, because water and labor spent on a field that would never yield is water and labor stolen from one that would. Third, it **irrigated the few that yielded** — scarce resources were concentrated on the acres already setting grain, not spread thin across every hopeful sprout. And fourth — the one every anxious lord skips — it **weighed the crop at the scale and wrote the number in the book.** An estate that never weighed its harvest could not tell a good year from a ruinous one, could not learn which fields to plant again, and could not answer the only question the creditors ever asked at the door: *what did the land return?*

Here is the part the board must sit with. For a month you have built the finest agricultural estate in the county — you metered the water, sorted the fields, walled the boundary, tagged every hand who works the land. But this season you planted a thousand pilots and weighed almost nothing. The green is spectacular: agents in every department, demos in every board pack. And the numbers at the granary door are brutal — **ninety-five rows in a hundred set no grain at all** (MIT), **eighty-nine seedlings in a hundred never reach the harvest** (Gartner), and only **four estates in a hundred even bring a scale to the board** (Deloitte). The seed you fretted over is about to be given away for nothing (Kimi K3, 5 days). It was never the seed. It was always the harvest — the outcome you plant each agent against, the dry rows you have the discipline to pull, the few yielding acres you pour the water onto, and the weighed bushel you carry to the board's scale.

**The moral:** when the seed is free, do not measure the estate by how much it plants — planting is the cheap part now, and a field of pilots is not a crop. Measure it by what reaches the scale. **Plant every agent against a named P&L outcome and a named owner.** **Tend and thin** — kill the dry pilots early, before they drink another season's budget. **Irrigate the few that yield** — concentrate resources on the minority already reaching production, where the return is real. And **weigh the crop and write it in the book** — carry a P&L number, not a demo, to the board, because an estate that never weighs its harvest cannot tell abundance from ruin.

**The question it forces:** *We have planted a whole field of pilots — but can we name, for every agent, the P&L outcome it was sown against and who owns that outcome? Have we pulled the dry rows, or are we still watering them? And when the board asks what the land returned, do we carry a weighed bushel to the scale — or a photograph of a green field?*

## 4 · Technical Deep-Dive — The Seed, the Yield Gap, and the Scale

Strip the week's coverage down and the claim is financial, not technical: **when the seed is free, the governed object is no longer the model — it is the harvest, and the scale you weigh it on.** Read the reckoning as a farm in three parts — the seed (the model, commoditizing to nothing), the yield gap (why sown pilots don't become sold grain), and the scale (the outcome ledger you carry to the board).

- **The seed (the model — commoditizing to free).** Kimi K3's full open weights publish in **5 days**, self-hostable by any firm or government; the frontier that cost a fortune a year ago is becoming a giveaway. A free seed is not a harvest — it lowers the cost of *planting* to almost zero, which is precisely why estates are now drowning in green pilots and starving for weighed bushels. When the input commoditizes, the value migrates entirely to what you do with it.
- **The yield gap (why pilots don't scale — and it isn't the model).** MIT's diagnosis is the whole engineering story: *"pilots lie — they run on clean data, isolated systems and goodwill; production introduces ownership, security, integration, edge cases and accountability."* That is why **95%** show no P&L and **89%** never reach production. The gap between a green field and sold grain is exactly the production layer this radar spent a month building — metering, routing, the MCP plane, the authorization ladder, the identity passport. Those layers are the irrigation and fencing that carry a planting to harvest. But irrigation on a field sown for no market is just expensive mud: the stack only pays when each agent is planted against a **named P&L outcome with a named owner.**
- **The scale (measurement — the discipline every anxious lord skips).** Only **4%** of organizations report AI value to the board (Deloitte); **fewer than one-third** of decision-makers can name a specific financial outcome from AI (Gartner). This is an estate that never weighs its crop — it cannot tell a good year from a ruinous one, cannot learn which fields to plant again, and cannot answer the creditor at the door. The fix is an **outcome ledger:** every agent tied to a P&L line (cost taken out or revenue added), an owner, a baseline and a weighed result — the financial counterpart to the audit manifest this radar built last week.

The strategic core: **a harvest is a moat if every planting is sown against a market, thinned without sentiment, irrigated where it yields, and weighed on the board's scale — and a money pit if you plant everything, water everything, and weigh nothing.** After Kimi K3, "we have agents in every department" is not a defense; *"here is the P&L each agent returned, the dry rows we pulled, and the number we carried to the board"* is.

### Why a free seed makes the harvest the binding constraint

The forces this radar has tracked all month — commodity models (Kimi K3, 5 days), standard plumbing (MCP, 6 days), cheap routing, graduated authority, attested identity — all lower the cost of *planting* toward zero. That is exactly why the field is now choked with green pilots: when sowing is nearly free, everyone over-plants, and the scarce discipline becomes *harvesting* — thinning, irrigating the yield, and weighing the crop. A cheaper seed does not shrink your governance problem; it multiplies your unweighed fields. The binding constraint on value is no longer model access — it is the estate's ability to tie each planting to a market, pull the dry rows, and carry a weighed bushel to the board.

## 7 · So What — Three Moves for the Next 30 Days

1. **Plant for the market — tie every agent to a P&L outcome and an owner before you sow.** No new agent enters production without a named financial line (cost taken out or revenue added), a baseline, and a single accountable owner. This is the discipline that separates the **5%** extracting real value from the **95%** stuck in green. Beware **agent washing** on the buy side: of thousands of "agentic" vendors, Gartner counts ~**130** that truly deliver, so audit new seed before you plant it.

3. **Irrigate the yield and weigh the crop.** Concentrate spend on the ~**11%** already reaching production, where the return is real (~171% ROI, as reported; ~4-month payback in customer service). Then build the **outcome ledger** — every agent to a P&L line, an owner, a baseline and a weighed result — and carry that number, not a demo, to the board. When the seed is free, the estate is judged by bushels delivered to the scale, not acres of green; the granary discipline is the whole point of the stack you spent a month building.
