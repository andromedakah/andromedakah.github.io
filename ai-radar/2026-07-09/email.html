Hired Swords

Thursday, 9 July 2026 · Cross-sector edition · C-level + Engineering

In five weeks OpenAI, Anthropic, Microsoft and AWS committed billions not to bigger models but to forward-deployed engineers — specialists embedded inside your teams to write production code in your systems. The bottleneck moved off the model and onto the humans who deploy it. If the sword that wins your battles belongs to someone else, whose kingdom is it?

1 · Executive Summary

The center of gravity moved off the model and onto the humans who deploy it. In ~5 weeks the whole supply side committed billions to forward-deployed engineering (FDE) — engineers embedded in your teams, writing production code in your systems. AWS put $1B into an FDE org on 30 June; Microsoft's Frontier Company is $2.5B / ~6,000 engineers. Both follow the labs: OpenAI's DeployCo ($4B+) and Anthropic's Applied AI JV (~$1.5B). The SIs are in too (Accenture/ServiceNow, EY).

1. The bottleneck was never the model. "Forward deployed engineers are the invoice for making AI real." The constraint is data integration, workflow redesign and change management in messy legacy estates — why ~95% of pilots move no P&L and 56% of CEOs see no financial benefit.

2. When your model vendor becomes your SI, you've hired a mercenary army. FDEs win the battle — but rented from the firm that sells the model, they see your proprietary workflows, hold the know-how, and deepen a dependency they profit from. Your own people build no muscle.

3. The move is capability transfer, not rental. Own what compounds, rent what commoditizes. Make every engagement transfer the craft: your runbooks, your knowledge graph, your evals, trained internal champions.

Bottom line: the question isn't "which vendor will deploy AI for us?" — they're all lining up. It's "will this engagement leave us abler, or just more dependent?" Take the help — and make capability transfer a written contract term.

2 · Allegory — The Mercenary Army

A rich Renaissance republic grew too busy to train its own soldiers, so it hired condottieri — brilliant mercenary companies for rent. They won every battle. Machiavelli wrote the warning: mercenaries are "useless and dangerous," because their skill is real but their loyalty is to the purse.

The hired company learns your walls and the roads to your treasury; your own people forget how to fight; and the day the payment lapses, the army you leaned on is the army at your gate. The forward-deployed engineer is the condottiero; the model vendors just became the mercenary captains, renting you their swords. If the only people who understand how AI runs in your business are on someone else's payroll, you've rented your defense from the party most able to hold it hostage.

The question it forces: When the vendor's engineers ride out of our most important systems, do our own people know how to fight — or have we financed a dependency we'll never stop paying?

3 · Technical — The Bottleneck Moved to the Humans

FDE (pioneered ~20 yrs ago at Palantir) embeds engineers who write production code in your systems. The model is a commodity input; the value gap is the last mile — data, workflow, governance, evals, change management. The hazard is who closes it: rent the deployment layer from the party that sells the model, and you re-create lock-in one layer higher, in the humans.

Rent the sword (trap)Vendor staff operate it; they see your workflows; know-how leaves at exit.
DependencyLock-in one layer higher — hardest to unwind, and they profit from it.
Transfer the craft (win)Runbooks, knowledge graph and evals are yours; internal champions trained.
Own FDE functionStaff your own; the engagement leaves you abler, not just dependent.

On legacy: same seam this radar keeps arriving at — own what compounds, rent what commoditizes. The deployment layer is the human counterpart to the gateway (4 Jul identity, 5 Jul deployment, 6 Jul metering, 7 Jul routing, 8 Jul MCP). Rent the sword to win the war; spend the peace training your own army.

5 · So What — 3 Moves for the Next 30 Days

1. Make capability transfer a written contract term. The deliverable can't only be a working system — it must be runbooks, an owned knowledge graph, portable evals, and trained internal champions you keep. Otherwise you're financing a dependency, not buying a capability.

2. Stand up your own forward-deployed function. Treat internal applied-AI engineering as a strategic hire, not an outsourced line item. Even a small in-house FDE team that shadows every vendor engagement converts rented craft into owned muscle.

3. Scope what the hired sword sees — and audit it. Define what proprietary terrain vendor engineers access, who owns the resulting models and data, and how accountability is demonstrated — especially with EU AI Act GPAI enforcement live on 2 August.
