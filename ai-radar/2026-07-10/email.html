The Switchboard

Friday, 10 July 2026 · Cross-sector edition · C-level + Engineering

OpenAI shipped GPT-5.6 — its cheapest tier, Luna at $1/$6, is built for exactly what an agent does all day. A day earlier Gartner priced the fallout: $234B of enterprise-app spend, ~20% of SaaS by 2030, exposed to "agentic arbitrage" as agents do the work across your systems and the software goes invisible. When the switching goes automatic, are you still paying per operator — and do you own the exchange?

1 · Executive Summary

Two data points rhyme into one warning. On 9 July OpenAI made GPT-5.6 GA in three tiers — Sol ($5/$30), Terra ($2.50/$15, ~2× cheaper than GPT-5.5), Luna ($1/$6). Luna is built for classification, extraction, routing and first-pass drafting — the primitives an agent uses to move work across systems. A day earlier, Gartner priced the consequence: $234B of enterprise-app spend — ~20% of SaaS by 2030 — exposed to "agentic arbitrage," as agents bypass the interface and make software invisible.

1. The interface is disrupted, not the work. When one AI-equipped worker does the work of five, you renew fewer seats. The early-2026 "SaaSpocalypse" already erased ~$285B in software value. Luna pricing is what makes an agent cheaper than a seat.

2. Value migrates to the network. Gartner's counsel: move from interface-based to outcome-based value, preserve "customer-specific knowledge," and note that AI-native players win by becoming the orchestration layer. Value moves to the system of record, the data, the orchestration.

3. Own the exchange, not the chairs. Re-underwrite renewals as a seat question, push to outcome/usage terms, and keep the data and orchestration layer yours. Gartner: ≥40% of SaaS spend shifts to usage/agent/outcome pricing by 2030.

Bottom line: stop asking "which SaaS seats do we renew?" Ask "as the interface goes invisible, do we own the network the work routes through — or are we paying per operator at a switchboard no human will sit at?" The switching went automatic. Own the exchange.

2 · Allegory — The Switchboard Operators

For half a century, every phone call was connected by a human. Telephone companies ran vast halls of switchboard operators, and priced themselves per operator's chair. Then automatic switching arrived — and calling didn't decline, it exploded. What collapsed was the per-operator model. The operators were never the network; they were the interface to it.

Seat-based SaaS is the switchboard operator; the agent is automatic switching; GPT-5.6's Luna ($1/$6) is the cheap current that makes it run at scale. When an agent does the work across your systems, no human opens the app — the seat connects nothing. Gartner put the meter on it: $234B, ~20% of enterprise-app SaaS by 2030. The work isn't leaving; the interface is. The money migrates to whoever owns the exchange.

The question it forces: As agents do the work across our systems, are we still paying per seat for interfaces no human will open — and do we own the exchange the work routes through, or just rent chairs in someone else's hall?

3 · Technical — The Interface Goes Invisible

GPT-5.6's tier ladder (Luna for cheap routing/extraction, Terra for balanced work, Sol for the hard steps) is the cost curve that makes agentic arbitrage economical. The frontier is a commodity; the value gap is the interface dissolving into agents. The hazard is who owns the new switchboard — rent the orchestration layer from the party doing the arbitrage and you swap a per-seat bill for a per-outcome dependency.

5 · So What — 3 Moves for the Next 30 Days

1. Re-underwrite every SaaS renewal as a seat question. Model where agentic arbitrage collapses seat counts and push to outcome/usage terms now — Gartner sees ≥40% of SaaS spend moving that way by 2030. Don't lock into per-seat deals for a switchboard no human will sit at.

2. Own the exchange, not the chairs. Value migrates to the system of record, the data and the orchestration layer. Require the agents routing across your stack to run on data and infrastructure you own — that's where the $234B goes.

3. Turn agentic arbitrage on your own cost base. Luna pricing ($1/$6) makes agents on high-volume cross-system work economical today. Pilot one workflow where an agent replaces the interface — measure outcome, not seats — before a vendor books the saving as margin.
