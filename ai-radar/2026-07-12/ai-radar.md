# 🛰️ AI Tech Radar — The Recipe Walks Out

**Sunday, 12 July 2026 · Cross-sector edition · Audience: C-level + Engineering**

> For two weeks this radar has argued the same thing from every angle: the model is commoditizing, and the only thing that survives commoditization is what *you* own that no one can copy — your proprietary know-how. On **10 July that thesis walked into a federal courtroom.** **Apple sued OpenAI, Jony Ive's io Products, and two former Apple employees** in the Northern District of California, alleging a coordinated scheme to steal Apple's confidential product designs, manufacturing processes and supply-chain strategies — a scheme Apple says operated "at every level." The details are a masterclass in how a moat actually leaks: a departing VP allegedly used Apple's secret project code names in recruiting and asked candidates to bring Apple hardware to interviews; an engineer allegedly kept his Apple laptop and downloaded confidential docs; and **more than 400 former Apple employees now work at OpenAI.** The most secretive company in technology just discovered its secrets don't leave through the firewall. They leave on two legs. The board question is no longer *how do we build the moat?* It is: ***the one asset that doesn't commoditize has no walls — what is our plan to keep it from walking out the door and into the browser?***

## 1 · Executive Summary (90-second read)

The commoditization thesis got its courtroom test. On **10 July 2026 Apple filed suit in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California** against **OpenAI, io Products (Jony Ive's hardware venture), and two former Apple employees** — ex-VP of product design Tang Yew Tan and former senior systems electrical engineer Chang Liu — alleging trade-secret theft and breach of contract. The complaint describes a "coordinated" effort to extract Apple's confidential designs, manufacturing processes and supply-chain strategies, running "at every level": Tan allegedly used Apple's confidential project code names during OpenAI recruiting and asked candidates to bring in Apple hardware components; Liu allegedly failed to return his Apple laptop and used it to download confidential technical documents. Apple says **more than 400 former Apple employees now work at OpenAI.** OpenAI's response: *"We have no interest in other companies' trade secrets."* Whatever the merits, the case is a live demonstration of the one enterprise-AI lesson that keeps compounding — **the asset that doesn't commoditize is proprietary know-how, and it has no firewall.**

1. **Your moat lives in heads and documents, not in a vault.** Apple is the most secrecy-disciplined company in tech, and its complaint reads as an admission that the discipline still leaked — through hiring, through an unreturned device, through code names spoken in interviews. This is not exotic. **59% of departing employees take confidential company data with them; 70% of insider IP theft happens within 30 days of a resignation; 37% of organizations report IP loss when an employee leaves** (all as reported). The AI talent war has turned ordinary attrition into a strategic exfiltration channel.

2. **The AI era opened a second door — the browser.** Even the staff who stay leak. **77% of employees now paste data into GenAI tools; 26.4% of file uploads to those tools contain sensitive data; and GenAI has become the #1 channel for corporate data exfiltration, ~32% of all unauthorized data movement** (LayerX, as reported). Of the technical data pasted, a majority is proprietary source code. Your most valuable, least-copyable knowledge is being handed to third-party models every day — usually with good intentions and no policy.

3. **The move is to govern the recipe, not just guard the vault.** You cannot lock a mind, but you can govern how knowledge is created, accessed and carried out: least-*knowledge* access (not just least-privilege), a real offboarding protocol with device return and access revocation, data-loss controls on the GenAI browser, provenance/watermarking on your crown-jewel IP, and clean contractual hygiene (assignment, non-disclosure, indemnity). That is also where the **EU AI Act** points — provenance, documentation and accountability — with GPAI enforcement live **2 August 2026.**

**Bottom line:** stop asking *"how do we build a moat the model can't commoditize?"* You already have one — it's your proprietary know-how. Start asking *"that moat has no walls; it walks out on two legs and through the browser — do we govern how it leaves, or are we just hoping our people are loyal and our NDAs are scary?"* Guard the recipe, not just the vault.

## 2 · Allegory of the Day — "The Secret in the Cook's Head"

*Topic: On 10 July Apple sued OpenAI, io Products and two former employees for trade-secret theft, alleging a scheme "at every level" and noting 400+ ex-Apple staff now at OpenAI. The one asset that survives commoditization — proprietary know-how — has no firewall: it leaves through hiring, through devices, and (in the AI era) through the browser. You can lock a vault; you cannot lock a mind. You govern it.*

A great restaurant's whole value was one thing: a recipe no rival could reproduce. The owner built a magnificent kitchen, a heavy safe for the written formula, cameras on every door. He was proud of his security. But the recipe was never really in the safe — it lived in the **head of the cook**, refined over years of doing the work. The competitors across town understood this perfectly. They did not try to crack the safe. They hired the cook. And the sous-chefs. And the supplier who knew which farm, and the manager who knew the margins. One quiet season later, the rival's menu tasted familiar, and the owner's cameras had recorded nothing at all, because nothing was ever stolen through a door.

The restaurants that survived learned the lesson the hard way: **you do not protect a recipe by guarding the vault — you protect it by governing the kitchen.** They wrote down what could be written and owned it cleanly. They kept the truly irreplaceable knowledge distributed, so no single cook carried the whole formula out in his head. They ran a real departure ritual — return the keys, the notebooks, the phone — the day someone gave notice, not a week later. And they built a culture where people stayed because they were valued, because the one control no contract provides is a cook who doesn't want to leave.

The AI parallel arrived this week in a courtroom. **Apple is the owner with the beautiful safe.** Its complaint alleges the rival didn't break in — it *hired the cooks*: a VP who allegedly carried the code names in his head and the playbook in his hands, an engineer who allegedly walked out with the laptop, and 400 more who simply took new jobs. And there is a second door the old restaurant never had: today the cooks who *stay* also paste the recipe into a stranger's notebook every afternoon — **77% of employees feed company data into GenAI tools**, which have quietly become the number-one way corporate secrets leave the building.

**The moral:** the thing that makes you un-commoditizable is the thing you can least lock up. It lives in heads and documents, and it leaves on two legs and through the browser. You do not defend it with a bigger safe — you defend it with least-knowledge access, a real offboarding ritual, controls on the AI browser, provenance on the crown jewels, and a culture worth staying for. Guard the kitchen, not just the vault.

**The question it forces:** *Our durable advantage is the know-how no rival can copy — and it has no firewall. Do we govern how it's created, accessed and carried out, or are we, like the proud owner, admiring the safe while the cook packs his knives?*

## 3 · C-Level Engagement — Questions by Sector

### 🌐 Cross-sector (any boardroom)
- Our real moat is proprietary know-how, and **59% of departing employees take confidential data with them** (as reported). **Do we have an offboarding protocol** — same-day access revocation, device return, exit forensics on the highest-risk leavers — or does our security stop at the firewall while the recipe walks out the door?
- **77% of our people paste data into GenAI tools and 26.4% of uploads contain sensitive data** (as reported). **What stops an employee from handing our crown-jewel IP to a third-party model this afternoon** — a policy no one reads, or an actual control on the browser?
- Apple, the most secretive company in tech, is now *suing* to protect what leaked. **If Apple can't hold its secrets by discipline alone, what exactly is our plan** — and do we even know which knowledge, in which heads, is the un-copyable part?

### 🏦 Financial Services
- Alpha, models, pricing logic and client relationships are pure know-how that walks with the people who hold it — and **70% of insider IP theft happens within 30 days of a resignation** (as reported). **Do we watch data movement on the highest-privilege leavers**, or discover the loss when it shows up in a competitor's book?
- Traders and quants live in GenAI tools. **Can we prove no proprietary strategy or client data was pasted into a public model** — a question a regulator or an auditor may soon ask directly?

### 🧬 Healthcare / Life Sciences
- Research pipelines, trial designs and manufacturing processes are the moat, and a departing scientist carries years of it in their head. **Is our irreplaceable IP distributed and documented** so no single departure hands a rival the formula — and is every high-value dataset access-logged?
- Clinicians and researchers paste into GenAI daily. **Where is the control that keeps patient data and proprietary protocols out of a public model**, and can we evidence it for HIPAA and IRB oversight?

### 🏭 Manufacturing / Industrials
- Apple's complaint is literally about **manufacturing processes and supply-chain strategies** — the exact know-how that defines an industrial moat. **Do we treat process IP as a governed asset** — provenance, least-knowledge access, tight offboarding — or as tribal knowledge anyone can walk out with?
- Design and process engineers use GenAI to move faster. **What stops a CAD file, a tolerance spec or a supplier list from being uploaded to a third-party model** and leaving our control for good?

### 🛒 Retail / Consumer
- Pricing algorithms, supplier terms and customer data are the edge, and merchandising teams churn. **When someone leaves for a competitor, do we revoke access and check what they took the same day** — or trust the NDA to do work it can't?
- Marketing and category teams paste briefs, roadmaps and customer lists into GenAI tools constantly. **Have we put a control on that browser**, or is our go-to-market plan quietly training someone else's model?

### 🏛️ Public Sector / Regulated
- Citizen data, procurement strategy and security know-how leaving with departing staff is a national-interest problem, not just a commercial one. **Do we have exit controls and access revocation that actually fire on day one**, and can we demonstrate them?
- **EU AI Act GPAI enforcement takes effect 2 August 2026** (max €15M or 3% of global turnover), demanding provenance, documentation and human oversight. **Can we show a clean chain of custody for the data and know-how in our AI systems** — including what our people fed into third-party models?

## 4 · Technical Deep-Dive — The Moat With No Walls

The defining move of the week is not a product launch — it is a lawsuit that makes an abstract strategy concrete. For two weeks this radar has argued that as the model commoditizes, durable value concentrates in **what you own that can't be copied**: proprietary data, process know-how, customer relationships, the ontology and evals that encode your business. **Apple v. OpenAI is that thesis stress-tested in court** — and it exposes the uncomfortable corollary: *the asset that doesn't commoditize is also the asset with no firewall.* Trade secrets are legally the crown jewels, but they don't live behind a network boundary. They live in **heads** (the VP who allegedly recruited using secret code names), on **devices** (the engineer who allegedly kept his laptop), and — new this era — in the **browser**, where staff paste them into third-party models by the thousand.

**Why this is a governance event, not a celebrity lawsuit.** An IP moat leaks through three channels, and most enterprises defend only the first. **(1) The network** — firewalls, DLP on email, endpoint controls. Well-funded, and increasingly beside the point. **(2) The people** — departing employees carry knowledge out lawfully (in their heads) and sometimes unlawfully (in files and devices): 59% take confidential data, 70% of insider theft clusters around resignation, 37% of orgs report IP loss on departure (all as reported). **(3) The browser** — even loyal staff exfiltrate by convenience: 77% paste into GenAI tools, 26.4% of uploads carry sensitive data, and GenAI is now the #1 exfiltration channel at ~32% of unauthorized data movement (LayerX, as reported). Channel 1 is guarded; channels 2 and 3 are where the recipe actually walks.

### Why "how do we build the moat?" is the wrong question

You already have the moat — it's the know-how a competitor can't clone. The failure mode isn't lacking a moat; it's guarding the wrong wall. Apple built the beautiful vault and still filed suit, because the value was never behind the network — it was in the people and the processes, and those move. **The right question is "when knowledge leaves — with a departing employee or a pasted prompt — can we see it, prove it was ours, and slow it down?"** — measured in offboarding SLAs, GenAI-browser controls, IP provenance, and retention. That is where a moat is actually kept.

The retrofit isn't a new tool; it's discipline wired to your IdP and your HR system: least-knowledge access, an offboarding protocol that fires on day one, DLP on the GenAI browser, and provenance on the crown jewels. Skip it, and you're Apple — admiring the safe while the cook packs his knives.

**The clean mental model:** *Your moat is a recipe, and it lives in the cook's head. You don't protect it with a bigger safe — you govern the kitchen. ## 7 · So What — Three Moves for the Next 30 Days

1. **Map the recipe before you defend it.** You cannot govern know-how you haven't located. Run a crown-jewel inventory: which knowledge is genuinely un-copyable (designs, processes, pricing, models, customer intimacy), *which heads and documents hold it*, and how concentrated is it? Where one departure would hand a rival the formula, distribute it — least-*knowledge* access, not just least-privilege. This is the map every other control depends on.

2. **Fix the two doors most enterprises leave open.** First, make offboarding fire on day one: same-day access revocation, device return, and exit forensics on high-risk leavers — because 70% of insider IP theft clusters around resignation. Second, put a real control on the GenAI browser: DLP/guardrails on paste-and-upload to third-party models, because that channel is now the #1 way secrets leave. These two moves close the leaks that firewalls never touched.

3. **Make the moat provable — and worth staying for.** Tag and add provenance to your crown-jewel IP so you can demonstrate what's yours and see it move (also your EU AI Act chain-of-custody evidence). Tighten the contractual hygiene — assignment, non-disclosure, indemnity — but don't mistake paper for protection. The one control no contract provides is people who don't want to leave: retention *is* IP security. Guard the recipe by governing the kitchen, not by admiring the safe.
