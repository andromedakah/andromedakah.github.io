The Recipe Walks Out

Sunday, 12 July 2026 · Cross-sector edition · C-level + Engineering

For two weeks this radar has argued the model is commoditizing and value lives in what you own that no rival can copy. On 10 July that thesis walked into a federal courtroom: Apple sued OpenAI, Jony Ive's io Products and two former employees, alleging a scheme "at every level" to steal its designs, manufacturing processes and supply-chain strategies — with 400+ ex-Apple staff now at OpenAI. The most secretive company in tech just learned its secrets don't leave through the firewall. They leave on two legs — and, in the AI era, through the browser. The board question isn't how to build the moat. It's who is walking out with it.

1 · Executive Summary

On 10 July Apple sued OpenAI, io Products (Jony Ive's venture) and two former employees in the Northern District of California, alleging trade-secret theft and breach of contract — a "coordinated" scheme "at every level" to extract Apple's confidential designs, manufacturing processes and supply-chain strategies. Apple says 400+ former Apple staff now work at OpenAI. OpenAI: "We have no interest in other companies' trade secrets." The case is the commoditization thesis stress-tested in court — the asset that doesn't commoditize is proprietary know-how, and it has no firewall.

1. Your moat lives in heads, not a vault. 59% of departing employees take confidential data; 70% of insider IP theft happens within 30 days of a resignation; 37% of orgs report IP loss on departure (as reported). The AI talent war turned ordinary attrition into a strategic exfiltration channel.

2. The AI era opened a second door — the browser. 77% of employees paste into GenAI tools; 26.4% of uploads carry sensitive data; GenAI is now the #1 exfiltration channel (~32% of unauthorized data movement) — LayerX, as reported. Even loyal staff hand your least-copyable knowledge to third-party models daily.

3. Govern the recipe, not just the vault. Least-knowledge access, same-day offboarding with device return, DLP on the GenAI browser, provenance on crown-jewel IP. It's also where the EU AI Act points — provenance, documentation, oversight — live 2 Aug 2026.

Bottom line: stop asking "how do we build a moat the model can't commoditize?" You already have one — your know-how. Ask "that moat has no walls; it walks out on two legs and through the browser — do we govern how it leaves, or just hope our people are loyal?" Guard the recipe, not just the vault.

2 · Allegory — The Secret in the Cook's Head

A great restaurant's whole value was one recipe no rival could reproduce. The owner built a magnificent kitchen, a heavy safe, cameras on every door. But the recipe was never in the safe — it lived in the head of the cook. The competitors didn't crack the safe; they hired the cook, the sous-chefs, the supplier who knew the farm. One season later the rival's menu tasted familiar, and the cameras had recorded nothing, because nothing left through a door.

Apple is the owner with the beautiful safe. Its complaint alleges the rival didn't break in — it hired the cooks: a VP who allegedly carried the code names, an engineer who allegedly walked out with the laptop, and 400 more who took new jobs. And there's a second door the old restaurant never had: the cooks who stay paste the recipe into a stranger's notebook every afternoon — 77% of employees feed company data into GenAI tools.

The question it forces: Our durable advantage is the know-how no rival can copy — and it has no firewall. Do we govern how it's created, accessed and carried out, or are we admiring the safe while the cook packs his knives?

3 · Technical — The Moat With No Walls

An IP moat leaks through three channels, and most enterprises defend only the first: the network (firewalls, email DLP — well-funded, increasingly beside the point); the people (departing staff carry knowledge out in heads and on devices); and the browser (loyal staff paste into GenAI by convenience). The recipe walks through channels 2 and 3 — the ones no firewall touches.

Guard the vault only (trap)Firewall + NDA on paper; no offboarding ritual; GenAI browser wide open; crown jewels untagged.
"Who has the recipe?"No one knows — it walks out on two legs and through the browser.
Govern the kitchen (win)Least-knowledge access; same-day offboarding + device return; DLP on the GenAI browser.
Prove and defend itProvenance on crown-jewel IP + a culture worth staying for — see what's yours and see it move.

On legacy: same seam this radar keeps arriving at — own what doesn't commoditize (6 Jul metering, 7 Jul routing, 8 Jul MCP, 9 Jul deployment-labor, 10 Jul vanishing interface, 11 Jul night shift). Today closes the loop: the thing you own that doesn't commoditize is your know-how — and it walks out with the people you lose, and the browser of those who stay. The retrofit is discipline wired to your IdP and HR: least-knowledge access, day-one offboarding, DLP on the AI browser, provenance on the crown jewels.

5 · So What — 3 Moves for the Next 30 Days

1. Map the recipe before you defend it. Run a crown-jewel inventory: which know-how is genuinely un-copyable, which heads and documents hold it, how concentrated is it? Where one departure hands a rival the formula, distribute it — least-knowledge access, not just least-privilege.

2. Fix the two doors most enterprises leave open. Make offboarding fire on day one — access revocation, device return, exit forensics on high-risk leavers (70% of insider theft clusters around resignation). And put a real control on the GenAI browser — DLP on paste-and-upload to third-party models, now the #1 way secrets leave.

3. Make the moat provable — and worth staying for. Tag and add provenance to crown-jewel IP (also your EU AI Act chain-of-custody evidence). Tighten contracts, but don't mistake paper for protection: retention is IP security. Guard the recipe by governing the kitchen, not admiring the safe.
