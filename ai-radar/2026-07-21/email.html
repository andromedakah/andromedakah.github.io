Yesterday the value settled on the signet ring — the graduated grant of authority. 1 · Executive Summary

The governance thesis moves from authorization to identity. A grant of authority is meaningless if you can't prove who holds it — and this week the identity layer became a shipping architecture. Google's Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform gives every agent a unique, cryptographically attested identity on the SPIFFE standard, signs and logs every API call, data access and file operation to it, and enforces it with mTLS through an Agent Gateway — placed below the application tier as an infrastructure primitive. Reported break with rivals: Anthropic and OpenAI position governance at the application tier. The stakes: NHIs now outnumber humans ~45:1, and 109:1 in the average enterprise (from 82:1 a year ago), while 78% lack any policy to create or retire them. Beneath: Kimi K3 open weights 27 July (6 days), MCP final spec 28 July (7 days), EU AI Act GPAI enforcement 2 Aug (12 days).

1. A grant of authority is only as good as the identity that holds it. Yesterday's signet ring decided how much an agent may do; today's question is whose name, provably. Industry consensus: an agent is a first-class non-human identity — its own principal, cryptographically attested, short-lived at runtime, with the human preserved as the delegating subject via token exchange. Gemini Enterprise is the sharpest statement because it puts the check below the application tier.

2. The border, not the front desk. The whole argument reduces to where identity is verified. At the application tier (the front desk), the agent is already inside before anyone asks who it is. Below it (the border), with cryptographic identity and mTLS on every call — regardless of how the agent was built — an unidentified or expired traveler never crosses.

3. The population already inverted — and it's ungoverned. NHIs outnumber humans 45:1 to 109:1 (144:1 cloud-native); 78% have no policy to create/remove them; ~two-thirds have already been breached via a compromised NHI. And 96% of enterprises run agents but only 12% have a centralized platform to govern them (OutSystems).

Bottom line: from the signet ring (the grant) to the passport (the identity that carries it) and the border where you check it. "How capable is the model" is a commodity question; "can we prove whose identity took this action, and did we verify it at the border or the front desk" decides whether your roll is evidence or fiction. Issue every agent a passport. Name its human sponsor. Check it at the border. Keep the manifest — and be able to seize the passport.

2 · Allegory — The Passport

For most of history you could cross a frontier on your face and your word. Then realms grew crowded, and the crowd made the word worthless — too many travelers claiming to be someone they were not. So the sovereign issued a passport: it named the bearer and vouched for them, and it was verifiable — sealed and stamped, so a guard could authenticate it rather than believe it. Four disciplines made it trustworthy: it was issued by an authority (not self-declared), it named a sponsor, it expired, and it was checked at the border — by the guard at the gate, not by the innkeeper who merely recorded your name after you'd already arrived.

An autonomous agent is a traveler you've let loose in your realm — and there are now forty-five of them for every one of your people. Yesterday you set how much authority each traveler's seal could carry. But a seal is worthless if you can't verify whose hand wears the ring, and you can't verify it at the front desk — by then the agent is already inside, acting in your name. Gemini Enterprise is the passport-and-border system rebuilt for software: a cryptographically attested identity issued by the infrastructure (not self-asserted), naming its human sponsor through token exchange, short-lived by design, checked at the border — below the application tier, on every call, with mutual-TLS.

The question it forces: Every agent acts under some identity — is it issued and attested by our infrastructure, does it name a human sponsor, does it expire, and can we seize it? And do we verify it at the border — below the application tier, on every call — or only at the front desk, after the traveler is already inside our walls?

3 · Technical — The Passport, the Sponsor, the Border

When machines acting in your name outnumber your people 45-to-1, the governed object is the identity that carries authority, and the layer where you verify it. The passport (attested identity — the object): a unique, cryptographically attested identity on SPIFFE, every API call, data access and file op signed and logged to it — attested, not asserted, so an agent can't simply claim trust. The sponsor (the human, through delegation): the human preserved as the delegating subject via token exchange, so every act names both the agent and a responsible party. The border (where you check): identity and gateway placed below the application tier, enforced with mTLS on every call regardless of how the agent was built — the guard at the gate, not the clerk at the front desk.

Commodity models (Kimi K3, 6 days), standard plumbing (MCP, 7 days) and cheap routing all mean more agents, minted faster than anyone tracks — which is why the identity population inverted to 45:1–109:1. The danger is an agent wired in with an inherited, shared, never-expiring service credential, checked (if at all) at the app it hits. The retrofit: attested identities (SPIFFE-style), the human sponsor via token exchange, short-lived credentials, an Agent Gateway / mTLS check below the app tier, and a tested seize-the-passport revocation path — feeding the manifest that is your EU AI Act evidence in 12 days.

5 · So What — 3 Moves for the Next 30 Days

1. Take the census — inventory every traveler first. You can't issue passports to a crowd you can't count. List every agent and non-human identity acting in your name — human sponsor, scope, credential type, expiry — and rank by blast radius. Expect a shock: NHIs outnumber people 45:1 to 109:1, and 78% start from no policy at all. The census doubles as the opening page of your EU AI Act manifest, due in 12 days.

2. Issue real passports — attested, sponsored, expiring, seizable. Make every agent a first-class identity: issued and cryptographically attested by your infrastructure (SPIFFE-style), not a shared or self-asserted app-tier token; carrying its human sponsor via token exchange; short-lived by default; and revocable in minutes — a tested seize-the-passport path with a named owner. Kill the long-lived shared service accounts behind two-thirds of NHI breaches.

3. Move the checkpoint to the border. Decide, deliberately, where identity is verified — and move it below the application tier. Enforce agent identity with mutual authentication at an Agent Gateway / mesh, on every call, regardless of which app or vendor built the agent — the guard at the gate, not the clerk at the front desk. That single choice makes your manifest trustworthy.
