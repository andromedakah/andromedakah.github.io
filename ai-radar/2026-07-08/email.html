Behind the Wall

Wednesday, 8 July 2026 · Cross-sector edition · C-level + Engineering

The connector grew up. MCP's Enterprise-Managed Authorization went stable and a sweeping stateless spec lands 28 July — so agent tooling just became load-bearing enterprise infrastructure. But that maturity moves the security boundary out of the protocol and onto you. The socket got universal; the danger moved behind the wall.

1 · Executive Summary

The plumbing this radar keeps flagging became a utility. On 18 June 2026 MCP's Enterprise-Managed Authorization (EMA) extension reached "stable" — provision every connector centrally through your identity provider, one login, no per-app OAuth. Okta shipped first; Anthropic/Claude and VS Code on the client; Asana, Atlassian, Figma, Linear, Supabase and Slack on the server. Two weeks later the 2026-07-28 stateless spec — the biggest revision since launch — lets any server sit behind an ordinary load balancer.

1. Convenience became a control plane. EMA does for agent tools what SSO did for SaaS: access decisions move into the IdP admin console, giving one auditable trail across every connector — exactly the seam behind 4 Jul (agent identity) and 7 Jul (one governance plane).

2. The stateless rewrite transfers liability. The protocol no longer remembers who is who; it hands the client tracking identifiers and state. Analysts warn it "shifts critical security responsibilities from the protocol itself to developers and platform operators." You have a 12-month window.

3. The gateway is the panel, not an option. Standardization you don't govern is a bigger attack surface with better docs. Route every MCP call through one policy-and-audit gateway wired to your IdP: per-agent identity, egress control, header hygiene, immutable log.

Bottom line: "Can our agents reach the tools?" is trivial now. "Who owns the wiring behind the socket?" is the question. Adopt EMA, put a governed gateway in front, staff the panel — before the 28 July spec and the 2 August regulator arrive.

2 · Allegory — Behind the Wall

When electricity was young, every device was hand-wired — connecting anything was an expert act. Then the world standardized the wall socket: plug in, and it works. That's the delight of EMA — one login and every tool your agent needs is simply there, "pretty magical."

But the moment the socket went universal, the danger didn't vanish — it moved behind the wall: the wiring, the fuse box, the load rating, and who's liable when the circuit overloads. EMA is the universal socket; the stateless spec is the day it went load-bearing; the security boundary the protocol used to hold now lives in identifiers and headers you own.

The question it forces: We're plugging every agent into a universal socket. Who owns the wiring behind it — the identity, the audit log, the load rating — or did we standardize connection and leave the fuse box unbuilt?

3 · Technical — When the Adapter Becomes the Foundation

EMA stable (18 Jun) centralizes authorization in your IdP with one audit trail; the 2026-07-28 spec removes the session handshake so any instance serves any request behind a load balancer. The old world held the boundary for you; the new world hands you the wiring:

Socket (the win)Login once via IdP; every connector wired; one policy + audit trail.
StateClient-held identifiers → predictable = hijack / cross-tenant risk.
HeadersMcp-Method / Mcp-Name → confusion + key/token/PII leakage.
Identity + egressPer-agent identity and header hygiene are now yours to issue.
AuditImmutable log lives with you, behind a governed gateway.

On legacy: the panel is a gateway retrofit — the same box as 4 Jul's agent identity, 5 Jul's deployment layer, 6 Jul's spend meter, 7 Jul's model-neutral router. EMA is the universal socket; the stateless spec made it load-bearing. The socket is the easy part — own the panel.

5 · So What — 3 Moves for the Next 30 Days

1. Make your IdP the control plane — adopt EMA on purpose. The win is the single auditable trail and central policy. Require every MCP connector an agent can reach to be provisioned and revocable from the IdP console — that list is also your 2 Aug EU AI Act evidence.

2. Brief engineering on the boundary shift — budget the 12 months. Client-held state identifiers and new headers are now yours. Review the stateless model against your threat model: unpredictable identifiers, header hygiene, cross-tenant isolation.

3. Put one governed gateway behind the socket. Route every MCP call through a policy-and-audit gateway wired to your IdP — per-agent identity, egress control, header hygiene, immutable log. Build the fuse box before you fill the building with sockets.
