The Standard Coupling

Friday, 17 July 2026 · Cross-sector edition · C-level + Engineering

Yesterday: keep your own scales. Today the plumbing beneath every enterprise agent becomes a public standard. In 11 days — on 28 July 2026 — the biggest revision of the Model Context Protocol (MCP) since its November-2024 debut goes final: a stateless core (any request lands on any server behind a plain load balancer), Enterprise-Managed Authorization already stable (18 June — one SSO login through your IdP, no scattered API keys), and MCP Apps (sandboxed interactive HTML UIs on the same audit path as a tool call). The connector is becoming boring, load-bearing infrastructure. But Akamai warns the same standard consolidates the attack surface — data exposure, phishing through trusted AI experiences, control-bypass, background-task abuse. A standard coupling standardizes the fitting, not the fire. Adopt the universal thread — but own where the water goes.

1 · Executive Summary

The commoditization thesis reaches the plumbing: the connective standard for agents stops being a moving target and gets bolted down. On 28 July 2026 — 11 days away — MCP's largest revision since November 2024 becomes final, an unmistakably enterprise release: stateless, identity-governed, UI-capable. It lands five days before the EU AI Act's mandatory GPAI enforcement (2 Aug 2026, 16 days). Two countdowns, one lesson.

1. The agent plumbing became enterprise-grade — and boring. The 2026-07-28 spec makes MCP stateless (no initialize handshake, no Mcp-Session-Id; context in _meta; any request → any instance). Enterprise-Managed Authorization went stable 18 June — one SSO login via your IdP, not personal keys in local configs. MCP Apps ship sandboxed HTML UIs on the same audit-and-consent path as a tool call. A protocol turning into infrastructure.

2. Standardization is a win and a consolidation of risk. When every hose couples to every hydrant, mutual aid scales — but a flaw in the shared coupling is everyone's. Akamai: the stateless state, MCP Apps and long-running Tasks create new avenues for customer-data exposure, phishing through trusted AI experiences, control-bypass and background-task abuse. A deprecation policy (12-month minimums) even standardizes how features leave — Roots, Sampling, Logging are on the clock.

3. The coupling never decides where the water goes — you do. MCP standardizes connection, identity and UI — never which tools an agent may reach, what data it may touch, or what it is for. Those are EU AI Act deployer duties, enforceable in 16 days. Gartner still expects 40% of enterprise apps to embed agents by end-2026 and >40% of agentic projects cancelled by end-2027 — the gap is governance, not plumbing.

Bottom line: adopt the standard — refusing it is the Baltimore mistake of owning a hose that couples to nothing. But don't confuse a standard connector for a safe deployment. Own what the coupling leaves you: the allow-list of servers and tools, least-privilege behind that single SSO login, the audit trail on every UI action, and the shutoff. The fitting is universal; the pressure is yours to set.

2 · Allegory — The Standard Coupling

On a February morning in 1904, a fire broke out in Baltimore. It should have been containable; it burned some thirty hours and took ~1,500 buildings. What turned a fire into the fire was a detail no one thinks about until it kills you: the couplings didn't fit. Engines raced in from Washington, Philadelphia and New York — and their hose threads wouldn't mate with Baltimore's hydrants. Willing help, working pumps, plenty of water, and firefighters standing in the street, unable to connect. Baltimore is why hydrants are standardized today: the disaster forced a national standard thread, so any engine could couple to any hydrant. That is exactly what MCP's 28 July spec does for agents — a boring, load-bearing win. An estate whose agents couple to nothing is the 1904 mistake.

But a standard coupling standardizes the fitting, never the fire. Three things stay your job: where the water goes (which tools and data each agent may reach), the pressure (least privilege behind that one SSO login), and who may open the valve (a universal connector is universal for an attacker too). The cities that survived the next fire adopted the common thread and kept drilling on the fireground.

The question it forces: When our agents run on a universal, stateless, SSO-governed connector, do we still own what the standard will never do for us — the allow-list, the least-privilege identity, and the audit-and-shutoff on every action — or have we mistaken a standard fitting for a safe deployment?

3 · Technical — What It Couples, What It Leaves You

Three couplings the standard makes. The thread (stateless core): no handshake, no Mcp-Session-Id, context in _meta, any request → any instance behind a plain load balancer. The hydrant key (Enterprise-Managed Auth, stable 18 Jun): SSO via your IdP (ID-JAG), no scattered keys — one high-value valve. The standard hose (MCP Apps): sandboxed HTML UI on the same audit/consent path as a tool call — rich, and a new phishing surface. What stays yours: where the water goes, the pressure, and who holds the key.

5 · So What — 3 Moves for the Next 30 Days

1. Adopt the standard — then map the fireground before you couple. Say yes to the 28 July spec, but first produce the one artifact the protocol won't: an allow-list of which MCP servers, tools and data each agent may reach. Where the water goes is your call — and your EU AI Act inventory evidence in 16 days.

2. Set the pressure behind the one SSO login. EMA centralizes the key, so least privilege matters more, not less. Scope permissions and add step-up behind that single login, and treat every MCP App like any third-party integration — review, sandbox, monitor, audit every UI action.

3. Write the shutoff and deprecation plan now. A universal connector is universal for an attacker too, and features are on a 12-month deprecation clock (Roots, Sampling, Logging). Document the kill switch and migration path, and keep a model- and tool-neutral abstraction (7 Jul) so no single coupling becomes lock-in.
