1 · Executive Summary

The commoditization thesis gets its first shipping proof point. Reporting over the weekend describes Microsoft's Project Perception, whose defining feature is an orchestration layer — it dispatches each vulnerability task to the cheapest-adequate model and escalates to a frontier model only for the hard steps. It is the model-neutral router (7 Jul) turned into a product, aimed at Anthropic's Claude Mythos. It lands amid the same countdowns: Kimi K3's open weights on 27 July (8 days), MCP's final spec on 28 July (9 days), EU AI Act GPAI enforcement on 2 Aug (14 days).

1. The first flagship on commodity intelligence competes on routing, not the model. Perception reportedly combines Microsoft, OpenAI and Anthropic models behind a layer that "chooses which AI model handles each step, rather than sending every job through the most powerful — and most expensive — system." Cheap model for triage/log-parsing; frontier model reserved for exploit chains, auth flows, production remediation — to make fixing cheap enough to run nonstop, undercutting Mythos 5 ($10 / $50 per 1M tokens, vetted partners only).

2. This is yesterday's "airframe," made concrete. The value isn't any single model — everyone can buy the same intelligence, and after Kimi K3 nearly download it. The value is the dispatch logic that knows which task is worth a frontier call and can swap a cheaper/open model in freely. As frontier capability converges, "enterprise AI wins or loses at the orchestration layer." Perception is that thesis shipped.

3. A routing layer is also a governance layer — or it isn't, and that's the trap. The point that picks the cheapest model is the natural place to enforce policy: which model sees which data, what's logged, where a human signs off. Build a pure cost-router and you optimize spend while multiplying an ungoverned surface — the one the EU AI Act inspects in 14 days.

Bottom line: yesterday the metal went cheap; today the first product proved where the value went — the triage tent, not the surgeon. When every competitor can buy or download the same frontier intelligence, your moat is the layer that decides which intelligence touches which job, reserves the expensive one for where it earns its keep, and enforces the governance the regulator demands on 2 August. Own the routing; make it govern as well as it saves.

2 · Allegory — The Triage Tent

On the Napoleonic battlefields, a wounded soldier's fate was decided less by any one surgeon's skill than by a colder idea: who gets seen, and in what order. Before Dominique-Jean Larrey, surgeon-in-chief of the Grande Armée, the wounded were treated by rank or chance, and the scarce hours of a skilled surgeon were spent almost at random. Larrey's innovation wasn't a sharper scalpel — it was a system of sorting (from the French trier) that assessed every casualty by the severity of the wound alone. The lightly hurt were patched; the surgeon's irreplaceable hands were reserved for the cases that would actually change the outcome. Paired with the "flying ambulance," it let a handful of surgeons at Borodino (1812) do the work of many — because the scarce resource was spent only where it was worth spending.

The part the board must sit with: triage didn't make the surgeon less necessary — it made the surgeon's scarce time enormously more valuable. The genius, copied into every army and ER on Earth, was the sorting, not the surgery. Project Perception is a triage tent for machine intelligence: frontier capability is no longer the scarce surgeon it was a year ago, but the best model on the right step still costs real money. The value it competes on is the triage — route the routine mass to a cheap model, reserve the frontier model for the wound that will actually kill the patient — and, the half most skip, govern the sorting itself, because Larrey's list of who-gets-the-surgeon was also a record of every decision made.

The question it forces: If every competitor can now buy or download the same frontier intelligence, is our advantage the model we license — a commodity — or the triage tent we build around it: the layer that decides which model touches which job, reserves the expensive one for where it earns its keep, and keeps the auditable record of every routing decision the regulator will demand on 2 August?

3 · Technical — The Surgeon, the Sorting, the Ledger

Perception bets on one claim: once intelligence is abundant, the value is in the routing, not the model. The surgeon (frontier capability, still costly): Mythos 5 lists at $10/$50 per 1M — rationed, called only for exploit chains, auth flows, production remediation. The sorting (the product): an orchestration layer across MS/OpenAI/Anthropic that routes the routine mass to a cheap model — making fixing cheap enough to run nonstop, and the model interchangeable. The ledger (governance): the one place that sees every task and escalation — build it to enforce and log policy and the router is your EU AI Act evidence; build it as pure cost-optimizer and it's ungoverned sprawl that happens to be cheap.

5 · So What — 3 Moves for the Next 30 Days

1. Instrument your AI workloads, then triage them. Most tasks don't need your most expensive model. Tag every AI call by whether it genuinely needs frontier capability, and route the routine mass (triage, parsing, drafting) to a cheap or — after 27 July — self-hostable open model via your model-neutral router (7 Jul). The triage logic becomes the asset, not the model.

2. Make your router govern, not just save. The orchestration layer sees every task and model choice — so make it enforce policy, not only price. Wire data-eligibility, logging and human-sign-off into the routing itself, so the same layer that saves money produces your EU AI Act evidence. A cost-only router builds an ungoverned surface the regulator inspects on 2 August.

3. Name the layer where your value lives. "We have the best model" is not a moat; the orchestration and the proprietary data and rules that steer it are. Write down, in one page, your triage tent — routing logic, escalation rules, the data that makes them smart, the audit ledger — and move budget from renting model capability toward compounding that.
