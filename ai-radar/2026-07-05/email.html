The Model Was the Easy Part

Sunday, 5 July 2026 · Cross-sector edition · C-level + Engineering

In one week, the two biggest names in AI stopped selling you a model and started selling you a way to make it pay. The hard part was never the intelligence — it's the last mile to P&L.

1 · Executive Summary

For three years the enterprise AI question was which model? This week the biggest players answered a different one — who will actually make it work inside your business? — with their balance sheets. The frontier moved downstream: from the lab to the last mile.

1. The model makers became consultants. On 2 Jul 2026 Microsoft formalized Frontier Company — $2.5B, ~6,000 engineers — to embed inside customers and wire AI into real workflows. In the same window, OpenAI's Frontier + Frontier Alliances (BCG, McKinsey, Accenture, Capgemini) launched. When the model makers sell the deployment, the model race isn't where the money is.

2. A brutal ROI reckoning. MIT Project NANDA: 95% of GenAI pilots have zero measurable P&L impact. PwC: 56% of CEOs saw no revenue/cost benefit. McKinsey: 73% ROI-failure. The gap isn't intelligence — it's the deployment layer.

3. "Own it, don't rent it." Palantir + Nvidia shipped a sovereign, air-gapped stack (Nemotron on Blackwell Ultra) to own weights and data. Karp called token billing a "wealth tax." The question shifts from which model to call to which model to own — and who captures the value.

Bottom line: the edge is no longer the biggest model — everyone can rent one. It accrues to whoever can embed intelligence into real workflows, govern it, and prove the P&L.

2 · Allegory — The Gym That Started Selling Trainers

A town got a magnificent gym — the best equipment money could buy. Memberships sold out in a week. A year later, 95 of every 100 members were no stronger: they'd bought the membership, walked the floor once, and drifted off. The machines were extraordinary. The problem was that owning a barbell has never made anyone strong.

So the owner stopped bragging about equipment and started selling trainers — people to wire the machines into a real program and put a number on your progress. The value was never in the iron; it was in the disciplined use of it. But the trainer kept the logbook — so some members hired their own and kept their own records.

The question it forces: Are we still buying gleaming equipment, or have we built the trainer — the deployment muscle that turns a model into a measured outcome — and do we still own the logbook?

3 · Technical — The Deployment Gap

Frontier intelligence is no longer scarce — you can rent a near-SOTA model by the token. Yet 95% of pilots never move the P&L. That's a deployment problem, not a model problem, which is why Palantir's forward-deployed engineering was copied by both big labs in a week. The five layers of the last mile — the work that turns a model into money:

1 · Context / DataWire the model to your systems of record & knowledge.
2 · WorkflowEmbed it in the real process, not a chatbot on the side.
3 · GovernanceIdentity, least-privilege, audit — trust it in production.
4 · EvaluationMeasure outcomes, not demos — a P&L number, not a vibe.
5 · Cost / MeterAttribute spend to value; kill the "wealth tax."

On legacy: the last mile is a legacy-integration problem. Forward-deployed engineering is the discipline of retrofitting — connecting a modern model to a decades-old estate via the identity fabric, cost-metering gateway, and RAG/MCP plumbing this radar keeps returning to. The model is the easy part precisely because the plumbing is the work.

5 · So What — 3 Moves for the Next 30 Days

1. Report a P&L number, not a pilot count. Put a revenue-or-cost figure next to your top five AI initiatives; the ones that can't produce one are your 95%.

2. Buy a capability, not a dependency. Run the rent-vs-own analysis on your most data-sensitive workflows; keep the reusable capability, weights, and logbook in any deployment contract.

3. Build the last-mile muscle. Stand up a small forward-deployed engineering function for the five layers, reusing your identity and cost-metering plumbing.
